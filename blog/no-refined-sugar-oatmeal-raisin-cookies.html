No Refined Sugar Oatmeal Raisin Cookies

Chewy, soft-centered oatmeal raisin cookies made without wheat flour and without refined sugar. Maple does the sweetening, oats do the structure, and a homemade gluten-free blend handles the rest.

Skip to recipe ↓

These are chewy, soft-centered oatmeal raisin cookies made without wheat flour and without refined sugar. Maple syrup does the sweetening. Oats do most of the structural work. And a simple gluten-free flour blend you can mix at home handles the rest.

One thing to know upfront: these cookies do not spread. More on why below, and what to do about it.

Make the Flour Blend

You can use a store-bought gluten-free all-purpose blend here, and it’ll work fine. But if you’d rather make your own (it’s cheaper and takes about thirty seconds), here’s the mix I use.

Makes 100g, enough for one batch:

IngredientAmount
Brown rice flour70g
Tapioca starch30g
Baking soda½ tsp
Xanthan gum¼ tsp

Whisk everything together, and it’s ready to use.

Making a jar of it? Multiply everything by five, whisk thoroughly, and store airtight. Kept dry, it holds for months, since baking soda is stable as long as it isn’t exposed to moisture or acid. Whisk the jar before each use so the soda and xanthan stay evenly distributed, then weigh out 100g per batch.

Using a store-bought blend? Use 100g, and check the label. If it already contains xanthan gum, don’t add more. Add ½ tsp baking soda separately, as most commercial blends don’t include leavening.

Why These Don’t Spread

If you’ve made conventional cookies, you expect a scooped ball to flatten and widen in the oven. These won’t, and there are two reasons.

Refined sugar drives spread. In a standard cookie, granulated sugar liquefies under heat, thinning the dough and letting it flow outward before the structure sets. Maple syrup is already liquid at room temperature, so it behaves differently.

Oats and xanthan gum resist flow. Both are aggressive at holding water. That’s what gives these cookies their chew, but it’s also what keeps the dough locked in place.

Flatten before baking, and you get the cookie shape you want, with a perfectly chewy texture.

Substitutions

Can I use banana instead of butter or oil? Partially. Banana brings moisture and sweetness, but no fat, so a straight swap gives you a cakey, soft cookie rather than a chewy one, and you lose the crisp edge entirely. If you want to try it, use 50g mashed banana plus 40g oil, and cut the maple to about 90ml since the banana sweetens too.

Other dried fruit? Chopped dried apricots, cranberries, or cherries all work at the same 120g. Raisins are the most forgiving because they rehydrate readily in the dough.

Can I use only oat flour instead of the blend? Yes, just use 100g oat flour or grind an additional 100g of oats. You’ll get a softer, more crumbly cookie without the chew that tapioca provides. Skip the xanthan if you go this route; oat flour binds well enough on its own.

Coconut sugar or dates instead of maple? Coconut sugar works at 120g, and because it’s a dry crystalline sugar, you’ll actually get slightly more spread than the maple version. Date paste works too but makes a denser cookie — use about 150g and add a splash of water to loosen.

On the oats: buy certified gluten-free. Oats are naturally gluten-free but are frequently processed on shared equipment with wheat, so standard oats carry real cross-contamination risk.

On the maple: use dark or Grade B if you can find it. The flavor is deeper and stands up better to baking than the lighter grades.

No Refined Sugar Oatmeal Raisin Cookies

Yield

~18 cookies

Prep

10 min

Rest

15 min

Bake

12–14 min

Equipment

  • Kitchen scale
  • Mixing bowl and whisk
  • Spatula
  • Cookie scoop
  • Flat-bottomed glass (for flattening)
  • Baking sheet and parchment

Ingredients

  • 150g certified gluten-free rolled oats
  • 100g gluten-free flour blend (above — already contains the baking soda and xanthan)
  • 120ml maple syrup
  • 120g raisins
  • 80g neutral oil or melted butter
  • 1 tsp vanilla extract (optional)
  • ¼ tsp salt (optional)

Method

  1. Combine the wet ingredients. Whisk the maple syrup, oil (or melted butter), and vanilla together in a bowl until smooth and fully emulsified. It should look glossy, not separated.
  2. Add everything else. Add the oats, flour blend, salt, and raisins. Stir until you have a cohesive dough. It’ll seem wet at first; that’s expected.
  3. Rest for 15 minutes. Don’t skip this. Oats and gluten-free flours absorb liquid slowly, and this rest is what separates a chewy cookie from a gritty one. The dough will noticeably firm up as it sits.
  4. Scoop and flatten. Scoop the dough onto a lined baking sheet. Then press each ball down with your palm or the flat bottom of a glass to roughly the thickness you want the finished cookie to be, about ⅓ to ½ inch.
  5. Bake. 350°F (175°C) for 12 to 14 minutes, until the edges are golden and the centers still look slightly underdone. They firm up as they cool.
  6. Cool on the sheet. Give them five minutes on the hot pan before moving them. Gluten-free cookies are fragile while warm and will break if you rush them onto a rack.

Storage

Store in an airtight container at room temperature for up to four to five days. They soften over time as the oats continue to absorb moisture, which most people prefer.

You can also freeze the raw scooped-and-flattened dough for up to three months. Just bake straight from frozen when you’re ready, adding two minutes to the total bake time.
