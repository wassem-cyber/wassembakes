DIY Yogurt Substitute for Baking (When You’re in a Pinch)

When a recipe calls for yogurt and you’re baking it, you don’t need yogurt. You need what yogurt does.

Here’s a truth most recipes won’t tell you: when a formula calls for yogurt and you’re baking it, you don’t actually need yogurt. You need what yogurt does for the batter.

Moisture. Fat. Structure. Tang.

That’s it.

The oven kills the probiotics anyway, so unless I’m building something raw or fermented, I’m not waiting a day to culture yogurt or running out to the store. I just engineer the four things I actually need.

We use this hack all the time at the bakery. Plant-based yogurt is expensive and hard to source consistently, and culturing our own takes a day or two we don’t always have. This is a lifesaver.

What yogurt is really doing in a baked recipe

Real yogurt brings fat (richness and tenderness), protein and viscosity (body and structure), and acid (tang, plus a reaction with baking soda for lift). If you can hit those four marks with shelf staples, you’ve got a working substitute.

Here’s how we do it at the bakery:

  • Coconut milk delivers the fat.
  • Psyllium husk builds the structure and water-binding that yogurt’s proteins normally provide.
  • Apple cider vinegar brings the tang and activates baking soda the same way yogurt’s lactic acid would.
  • Tapioca (optional) thickens it up to something closer to Greek yogurt if you want that heavier consistency.

Whisk it together and it goes straight into your batter.

The recipe

DIY Yogurt Substitute (makes ~420g — roughly 1¾ cups)

  • 400g coconut milk
  • 8g psyllium husk
  • 14g apple cider vinegar
  • 20g tapioca starch (optional — for a thicker, Greek-style consistency)

Whisk everything together until smooth. Keeps in the fridge for 4–5 days.

Flavor boost (optional but worth it): add a small scrape of vanilla bean paste and a pinch of salt. That’s what turns it from a functional substitute into something that actually tastes like real coconut yogurt.

Starter option (advanced)

If I need extra lift, I swap the vinegar for 35g of active sourdough starter:

  • 400g coconut milk
  • 35g sourdough starter
  • 8g psyllium husk

Mix with a spatula, cover, and rest in the fridge overnight.

A few warnings on this version. Don’t taste it raw — that’s live, unbaked starter, which means raw bacteria. It’s meant to go into the oven. Shelf life also drops considerably with starter in the mix, so make what you need and use it.

Honestly, I don’t reach for this version often. It’s an advanced move for when a recipe really needs the lift. The vinegar version handles 95% of what I’m doing.

When to use which

Use the vinegar version as your default — fast, reliable, neutral. Reach for the starter version only when you need real lift and you know what you’re doing with live cultures.

Either way, you’re not making yogurt. You’re engineering the parts of yogurt your recipe actually needs.

That’s the whole trick.
